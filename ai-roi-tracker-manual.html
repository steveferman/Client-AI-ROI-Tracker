1. New Project — Basic Info

These fields identify what you're testing and why. Found on the + Add New tab.

Project Name *

Required

What it's asking: A short, recognizable name for this AI implementation.

Why it matters: This appears on every card, chart, and export. Make it specific enough that you (or a client) can instantly recall what it refers to.

Good examples

  • "AI Email Drafting — Sales Outreach"
  • "Meeting Notes Automation"
  • "SOP Document Generation"

Avoid

  • "Test 1" — too vague to be useful later
  • "ChatGPT" — that's the tool, not the project

Description

What it's asking: A 1-2 sentence summary of what this AI implementation does.

Why it matters: Gives context when you revisit the project months later or when showing a client their portfolio. Think of it as the "elevator pitch" for this specific AI use case.

Example

"Using Claude to draft initial client proposal emails, then editing for tone and accuracy before sending."

AI Tool

What it's asking: Which AI product or service is being used.

Why it matters: Helps compare ROI across different tools. If you're testing Claude vs. ChatGPT for the same task, you'll want separate projects for each to compare performance.

Examples

Claude, ChatGPT, Copilot, Otter.ai, Custom GPT, Gemini, Midjourney

Process Replaced

What it's asking: The specific manual or legacy process that this AI is replacing or augmenting.

Why it matters: This is the "before" in your before/after story. It anchors the ROI calculation — you're comparing the AI way vs. this old way. Be specific about what was being done manually.

Examples

  • "Manual email writing and editing (2-3 drafts per email)"
  • "Handwritten meeting notes typed up after the meeting"
  • "Manual data entry from PDFs into spreadsheets"

2. Owner & Phase

These classify the project so you can filter and present data to the right audience.

Owner: Internal vs. Client

What it's asking: Is this an AI tool you're implementing for your own business, or for a client's business?

Why it matters: The dashboard can filter to show only internal projects or only a specific client's projects. When you're in a coaching session with a client, you filter to their name and present their ROI in isolation — powerful for demonstrating your coaching value.

When to choose each

InternalAI tools you use to run your business (email, SOPs, scheduling)
ClientAI implementations you're coaching a client through

Client Name

What it's asking: The name of the client this project belongs to. Only shows when Owner = Client.

Why it matters: Enables per-client filtering on the dashboard. You can show "Pam, here's the ROI across all three AI tools we implemented" in a single view.

Phase

What it's asking: Where is this implementation in its lifecycle?

PhaseWhat it means
Planning Evaluating tools, defining the use case, not yet live. No real measurements yet — you're estimating baselines and costs.
Pilot Actively testing the AI tool on a limited scope. This is where you start entering real measurements to see if the tool delivers value.
Scaling Proven value — now expanding to more use cases, more team members, or higher volume. ROI should be clearly positive.
Optimized Fully integrated into the workflow. Measurement is about maintaining and improving, not proving value.

Start Date

What it's asking: When did (or will) this AI implementation begin?

Why it matters: Used to calculate "months active," which drives breakeven analysis and amortizes one-time costs. Set this to when you first started using the tool, not when you created the project in the tracker.

3. Baseline (Before AI)

This section captures what life looked like before AI. These numbers are the "control group" that every ROI calculation compares against.

Key concept: If you don't know the baseline, you can't prove ROI. Spend time getting these numbers right — even rough estimates are better than zeros. Ask: "How long did this take before? What did it cost?"

Hours/week on manual process

What it's asking: How many hours per week did this task take using the old, manual way?

Why it matters: This is the time "before AI." When you later record measurements showing fewer hours, the difference becomes your time savings. This number gets multiplied by the hourly rate to produce a dollar value for time saved.

How to estimate

  • Track yourself for one normal week before starting the AI pilot
  • If already started, think back: "Last month without AI, how many hours?"
  • Include all time: doing the task, fixing mistakes, reviewing, etc.

Monthly cost ($)

What it's asking: What was the total monthly cost of doing this process the old way? This is separate from time — it covers tools, software, outsourcing, materials, etc.

Why it matters: If AI replaces a $500/mo tool or a $2,000/mo contractor, that's a direct cost savings. This field captures that "before" cost.

What to include

  • Software subscriptions the AI replaces (e.g., old transcription service)
  • Contractor or freelancer costs for this specific task
  • Materials, printing, or other direct costs

What NOT to include

  • Your own salary — that's captured in "hours" instead
  • Overhead like rent or internet — too indirect to attribute

Monthly revenue ($)

What it's asking: How much revenue was this activity generating per month before AI?

Why it matters: If AI helps you generate more revenue from the same activity (e.g., better sales emails = more closed deals), this is the "before" number to compare against. If the activity doesn't directly generate revenue, leave this at $0.

When to use this field

  • Sales outreach — revenue from leads contacted manually
  • Client proposals — value of deals closed from manual proposals
  • Content marketing — revenue attributable to manual content

Leave at $0 when

  • The task is internal (meeting notes, SOPs, data entry)
  • You can't reasonably attribute revenue to this specific activity

4. AI Investment

What you're spending to make the AI solution work. This is the "cost" side of the ROI equation.

Monthly tool/subscription cost ($)

What it's asking: The recurring monthly cost of the AI tool itself.

Why it matters: This is your ongoing investment. ROI must exceed this cost to be worthwhile. The app uses this to calculate breakeven — how many months until the benefits pay for the tool.

How to calculate

  • If one subscription covers multiple projects, divide proportionally
  • Example: $20/mo Claude Pro used 50% for email drafting = $10 for that project
  • Include API costs if applicable (estimate average monthly usage)

One-time setup cost ($)

What it's asking: Any upfront cost to get the AI solution working — integrations, custom development, consulting, hardware.

Why it matters: One-time costs affect breakeven timing. A $5,000 setup cost means you need more months of benefits before you're "in the green." The app amortizes this over the project's lifetime when calculating ROI.

Examples

  • Custom GPT/agent development: $500-$5,000
  • API integration with existing tools: $200-$2,000
  • Consultant setup fees
  • If no setup was needed, leave at $0

Training hours

What it's asking: How many hours were spent learning the AI tool, creating prompts/workflows, and onboarding team members?

Why it matters: Training time is a real cost that's easy to overlook. This gets multiplied by the hourly rate below to calculate the dollar value of the training investment. Ignoring this makes ROI look artificially high.

Hourly rate for training ($)

What it's asking: The blended hourly cost of the people who spent time learning the tool.

Why it matters: Multiplied by training hours to get the total training investment. Defaults to your global hourly rate setting ($75), but you can override it per project — e.g., if a senior exec spent time training, their rate might be higher.

How to estimate

Use the fully loaded cost (salary + benefits + overhead), not just base pay. A rough formula: annual salary / 2,080 hours. A $100K/yr employee = ~$48/hr base, ~$65-75/hr fully loaded.

5. Measurements — Time

Measurements are the ongoing data points you record (weekly or monthly) to track how the AI is actually performing. Found by clicking "+ Add Measurement" inside a project's detail view.

Tip: Set a recurring calendar reminder (weekly or monthly) to add measurements. Consistent data = accurate ROI. Even 5 minutes of tracking pays off in the story you can tell.

Date

What it's asking: The date this measurement covers. Usually the end of the period you're recording.

Why it matters: Drives the timeline on charts. Keeps measurements in order so you can see trends over time.

Period

What it's asking: Does this measurement cover a week or a month?

Why it matters: Helps you interpret the numbers. 10 hours saved per week is very different from 10 hours saved per month. Pick one cadence and stick with it for each project.

Hours WITH AI

What it's asking: How many hours did this task actually take this period, using the AI tool?

Why it matters: This is the "after" number. The difference between this and "Hours Without AI" = your time savings. Include all time: prompting the AI, reviewing output, editing, etc.

Hours WITHOUT AI (estimated)

What it's asking: How many hours would this task have taken this period if you'd done it the old way?

Why it matters: This is your counterfactual — "what would have happened without AI." It's pre-filled from your project baseline but you can adjust it. If this period had unusual volume (e.g., twice as many emails), increase the estimate accordingly.

Calculation

Time Saved = Hours Without AI - Hours With AI

6. Measurements — Cost

Track the direct dollar costs with and without AI for this period.

Actual cost this period ($)

What it's asking: What did it actually cost to perform this task this period, including the AI tool costs and any remaining manual labor costs?

Why it matters: This is the real-world cost with AI in place. Include the AI subscription/API fees, plus any remaining human labor or tools still needed.

Example

AI transcription service: $30/mo + remaining manual cleanup labor: $200/mo = $230 actual cost

Baseline cost (without AI) ($)

What it's asking: What would this task have cost this period if done entirely the old way?

Why it matters: Pre-filled from your project baseline. Adjust if this period's workload was different than usual. The difference between baseline and actual = your cost savings.

Calculation

Cost Saved = Baseline Cost - Actual Cost

7. Measurements — Revenue

Track whether AI is helping generate more revenue from this activity.

Actual revenue ($)

What it's asking: How much revenue did this activity generate this period with AI involved?

Why it matters: Captures the upside — AI doesn't just save money, it can help make money. Better proposals, faster responses, higher-quality outputs can all lead to more revenue.

Baseline revenue (without AI) ($)

What it's asking: How much revenue would this activity have generated this period without AI?

Why it matters: Pre-filled from your project baseline. The difference between actual and baseline revenue = your revenue impact from AI.

Calculation

Revenue Impact = Actual Revenue - Baseline Revenue

8. Measurements — Quality & Notes

Qualitative data that adds context to the numbers.

Quality Score (1-5)

What it's asking: Compared to the old manual process, how is the quality of the AI-assisted output?

1 — Much WorseAI output is significantly lower quality, requiring major rework
2 — Slightly WorseUsable but noticeably not as good as manual work
3 — SameEquivalent quality to what was produced manually
4 — BetterQuality improvement — more consistent, fewer errors, better formatting
5 — Much BetterSignificant quality improvement that wouldn't have been possible manually

Why it matters: ROI isn't just about speed and cost. If AI saves 5 hours but produces garbage, that's not a win. Quality scores help tell the complete story — especially when presenting to clients.

Notes

What it's asking: What happened this period? Any context that explains the numbers?

Why it matters: Numbers without context are hard to act on. Notes help you remember why a month was good or bad — "Holiday week, lower volume" or "Switched to new prompt template, big improvement."

9. Settings

Global configuration. Access via the gear icon in the top right.

Default Hourly Rate ($)

What it's asking: What is one hour of your (or your team's) time worth?

Why it matters: This is the multiplier that converts time savings into dollar value. If you save 10 hours and the rate is $75/hr, that's $750 in value. This single number has a huge impact on your reported ROI — set it honestly.

How to set it

  • Solo consultant: Use your billing rate (e.g., $150-300/hr)
  • Employee time: Use fully loaded cost (salary + benefits + overhead / 2,080 hrs)
  • Conservative approach: Use the lower end of the range — understating ROI is better than overstating it

Company Name

What it's asking: Your company name, displayed in the app header.

Why it matters: Branding. When you screen-share the dashboard with a client, your company name is visible. Defaults to "4 Pillar Coach, LLC."

Load Demo Data

What it does: Populates the app with 4 sample AI projects and measurement history so you can explore the interface without entering real data first.

When to use: Demoing the app to a prospect, learning how it works, or testing before entering real data. Warning: replaces any existing data.

10. How ROI Is Calculated

Transparency on the math behind the numbers. Every metric on the dashboard traces back to these formulas.

Per Measurement

Time Saved = Hours Without AI - Hours With AI

Time Saved ($) = Time Saved x Hourly Rate

Cost Saved = Baseline Cost - Actual Cost

Revenue Impact = Actual Revenue - Baseline Revenue

Total Benefit = Time Saved ($) + Cost Saved + Revenue Impact

Per Project (Aggregate)

Total Benefit = Sum of all measurement benefits

Total Investment = Setup Cost + (Training Hrs x Training Rate) + (Monthly Tool Cost x Months Active)

Net ROI = Total Benefit - Total Investment

ROI % = (Net ROI / Total Investment) x 100

Breakeven Analysis

Monthly Benefit = Total Benefit / Months Active

Monthly Net Benefit = Monthly Benefit - Monthly Tool Cost

One-Time Costs = Setup Cost + (Training Hrs x Training Rate)

Breakeven (months) = One-Time Costs / Monthly Net Benefit

If the breakeven number is less than your months active, you've already recouped your investment and are generating net value.

Dashboard Totals

All dashboard KPI cards are simply the sum across all filtered projects. The "Overall ROI %" is calculated as:

Overall ROI % = (Sum of All Net ROI / Sum of All Investment) x 100

11. Reading the Dashboard

What each dashboard element tells you at a glance.

KPI Cards (top row)

Hours SavedTotal hours reclaimed from manual work across all projects. This is time your team gets back.
Cost SavedDirect dollar savings from reduced tool costs, contractor fees, etc. Does NOT include monetized time.
Revenue ImpactAdditional revenue generated because of AI. Only shows if you're tracking revenue metrics.
Overall ROI %The big number. Green = positive return. Above 100% means you've more than doubled your investment.

ROI Trend Chart

Shows cumulative benefit over time, per project. Lines going up = growing value. Flat or declining lines = the AI tool may need re-evaluation.

Savings Breakdown (Donut)

Shows the proportion of value from each category: time savings (blue), cost savings (green), revenue impact (amber). Helps answer: "Where is AI creating the most value?"

Implementation Pipeline

Horizontal bar showing how many projects are in each phase. A healthy pipeline has projects distributed across phases, not all stuck in "Planning."

Top Performers

Projects ranked by ROI %. Your best-performing AI implementations rise to the top. Use this to identify what's working and replicate it.

Filters

Switch between "All Projects," "Internal Only," or "Client Only" to change what the dashboard displays. When set to Client, a second dropdown lets you select a specific client — perfect for 1-on-1 coaching sessions.

12. Tips for Accurate Tracking

Getting the most out of this tool.

1.

Measure the baseline BEFORE you start

Spend 1-2 weeks tracking the old process before implementing AI. Time yourself, record costs. This makes your "after" comparison credible.

2.

Be consistent with measurement cadence

Pick weekly or monthly and stick with it. Mixing periods within a project makes trends hard to read.

3.

Be honest with estimates

It's tempting to inflate "hours without AI" to make ROI look better. Resist this — credibility is more valuable than impressive numbers, especially with clients.

4.

Use notes religiously

Future-you will thank present-you. "Why did cost savings drop in March?" Your notes will have the answer.

5.

Export regularly

Data lives in your browser's localStorage. Export a JSON backup weekly. If you clear browser data, your projects are gone unless you have a backup.

6.

One project per AI use case

Don't lump "Claude for emails" and "Claude for SOPs" into one project. Different use cases have different ROI profiles and should be tracked separately.

7.

Update phases as you progress

Move projects from Planning to Pilot to Scaling to Optimized as they mature. The pipeline chart on the dashboard tells a powerful story about your AI maturity.

8.

Use it in client sessions

Filter the dashboard to a specific client before a coaching call. Screen-share and walk them through their ROI. This is one of the strongest retention and upsell tools in your coaching toolkit.
